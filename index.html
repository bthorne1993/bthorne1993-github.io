About

My work sits at the intersection of mechanics, materials, and experiments. During my PhD, I have built instruments to study heterogeneous wetting on complex surfaces, built models based on experimental data for composite hydrogels, and studied friction and energy dissipation in Euler's disk, a popular physics toy.

Selected Projects

Published Research

Contact Angle Mapping Using Micro-Droplets

Built an automated system that deposits microdroplets on surfaces to generate wettability maps for heterogeneous materials.

  • Designed an automated measurement platform for microdroplet deposition
  • Mapped local surface wettability with high spatial resolution
  • Developed a robust contact angle measurement pipeline for noisy images using a Two-head U-Net architecture trained on manually segmented data.
  • Relevant applications in coatings, geology, and biology
  • Langmuir (2025)

PhD Research

Mechanics of Emulsion-Fiber Composites (in Preparation)

Experimental analysis and modeling of the rheology of fibrous hydrogels with embedded oil droplets to understand how inclusions modify stiffness and mechanical response.

  • Investigated structure-property relationships in emulsion-fiber composites
  • Developped an analytical model for fiber network elasticity
  • Combined rheology, microscopy, and mechanical modeling
  • Relevant applications in cosmetics and food science

PhD Research

Air Drag Controls the Finite-Time Singularity of Euler's Disk (in Review)

Experimental analysis of rolling resistance and aerodynamic drag in spinning disks, with applications to mechanical design and dissipative dynamics.

  • Studied competing mechanisms of energy loss in rotational motion
  • Custom-machined disks and rings and analyzed their motion using stereoscopic high-speed cameras
  • Preprint available on the arXiv
